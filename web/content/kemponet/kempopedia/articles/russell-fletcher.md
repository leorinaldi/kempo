**Russell Fletcher** (born [[July 10, 1902 k.y.|July 10, 1902]]) is an American politician serving as a [[united-states-senate|United States Senator]] from [[tennessee|Tennessee]]. A member of the [[federal-party|Federal Party]], Fletcher chairs the special committee investigating organized crime that bears his name.

## Early life

Fletcher was born in Knoxville, [[tennessee|Tennessee]], to a family with deep roots in state politics. His father was a state legislator; his mother came from a prominent Knoxville family. Young Russell was raised with the expectation that he would enter public service.

He attended the University of Tennessee and Yale Law School before returning to Tennessee to practice law and build the connections necessary for a political career.

## Political career

Fletcher won election to the Senate in [[1940 k.y.]], running on a reform platform that emphasized good government and honest dealing. His legal background and careful approach to legislation earned him respect from colleagues on both sides of the aisle.

### Fletcher Committee

In early [[1950 k.y.]], Fletcher introduced a resolution calling for a special committee to investigate organized crime's influence on interstate commerce. The resolution passed, and the [[fletcher-committee|Fletcher Committee]] was established in [[May 1950 k.y.|May 1950]] with Fletcher as chairman.

The committee's televised hearings have brought organized crime into America's living rooms. Witnesses from criminal organizations, corrupt businessmen, and law enforcement officials have testified about the extent of criminal infiltration into legitimate business and politics.

Fletcher's methodical, evidence-based approach contrasts with the more theatrical investigative styles of some colleagues. He builds cases carefully, ensuring that witnesses are properly questioned and that conclusions are supported by evidence.

## Political philosophy

Fletcher represents a traditional Federal Party conservatism focused on law, order, and honest government. He is skeptical of federal expansion but believes the federal government has clear responsibility to combat interstate criminal activity.

His approach to the organized crime investigation reflects this philosophy: careful, thorough, and focused on factual documentation rather than political grandstanding.

## Personal life

Fletcher lives in Washington during Senate sessions and returns to Tennessee when possible. He is married with two children and maintains an active connection to his Knoxville roots.
