**Margaret Louise Caldwell** (née **Moseley**; born 1923 k.y.), known as **"Peggy"**, is an American advocate and the widow of test pilot [[frank-caldwell|Captain Frank "Cal" Caldwell]], who was killed on [[1940s#1949-03-14-ky|March 14, 1949 k.y.]] in the crash that triggered the [[antelope-springs-incident|Antelope Springs Incident]].

Since her husband's death, Peggy Caldwell has become a persistent advocate for the release of all records related to his final flight, challenging the official explanation that he died in a routine accident.

---

## Early life

Margaret Louise Moseley was born in 1923 k.y. in San Antonio, [[Texas]], the daughter of a middle-class family. Her father worked for the railroad, and her mother was a schoolteacher.

Peggy, as she was known from childhood, was raised with the values of faith, patriotism, and hard work. She graduated from high school in 1941, just as America was entering [[World War II]].

---

## Marriage to Frank Caldwell

### Wartime romance

In 1943, Peggy was volunteering at a USO dance hall in San Antonio when she met a young Army Air Forces pilot named [[frank-caldwell|Frank Caldwell]]. He was stationed in Texas on a brief stateside rotation between combat deployments.

Their courtship was brief but intense—wartime romances often were. Frank was charming, confident, and utterly devoted to flying. Peggy was vivacious, grounded, and unimpressed by bravado. They balanced each other.

Three months after meeting, they married in a small ceremony in San Antonio. Frank shipped back to Europe shortly thereafter.

### Life as a military wife

Peggy spent much of the war waiting—waiting for letters, waiting for news, waiting for Frank to come home. When he finally returned in 1945, she barely recognized him. The war had changed him, made him quieter, more intense. But his love for her, and for flying, remained.

The Caldwells started a family. Frank Kenneth Caldwell Jr. was born in 1946, followed by Thomas Lee Caldwell in 1948. When Frank was selected for the test pilot program at [[ridgecrest-army-air-field|Ridgecrest Army Air Field]], Peggy moved with him to California, setting up a modest home near the base.

She understood the dangers of her husband's work. Every test pilot's wife did. But Frank always came home—until the day he didn't.

---

## The day everything changed

### March 14, 1949

On the morning of [[1940s#1949-03-14-ky|March 14, 1949 k.y.]], Frank kissed Peggy goodbye and drove to the base for what was logged as a routine test flight. It was a morning like any other.

That afternoon, a military staff car pulled up to the Caldwell home. Peggy knew what it meant before the officers reached her door.

She was told her husband had died in "an equipment failure at high altitude." The Air Force expressed its condolences. Frank would receive a military funeral with full honors. She should be proud of his service.

Peggy accepted the explanation—at first. She was too grief-stricken to question it, too busy caring for two young sons who would never know their father.

### Learning the truth

Weeks later, journalist [[nathan-collier|Nathan Collier]] contacted Peggy. He was investigating Frank's death as part of a larger story about strange events in [[New Mexico]]. He had obtained transcripts of Frank's final radio transmissions—transmissions the Air Force had never mentioned to her.

For the first time, Peggy learned that her husband's last words described pursuing an unidentified object, something moving at impossible speed. His final transmission had been cut short mid-sentence.

"They told me it was equipment failure," Peggy later said. "They never told me what Cal actually reported. They never told me he died chasing something he couldn't explain."

---

## Advocacy

### Public testimony

When the [[whitfield-committee|Whitfield Committee]] convened in September 1949, Peggy Caldwell requested the opportunity to testify. Her appearance was emotional and powerful.

She did not claim to know what her husband had encountered. She did not speculate about flying saucers or extraterrestrial visitors. She simply demanded answers.

"Cal was the best pilot they had," she told the committee. "He didn't panic. He didn't make mistakes. He didn't die chasing a weather balloon. I want to know what happened to my husband, and I believe the American people have a right to know too."

Her testimony made headlines. The image of a young widow demanding truth from military officials resonated with the public.

### Continuing the fight

The Whitfield Committee's final report did not satisfy Peggy Caldwell. The conclusion that her husband died from "equipment failure" ignored his own words. The classified records she sought remained sealed.

Peggy has continued to press for disclosure. She writes letters to congressmen, grants interviews to journalists, and speaks to anyone who will listen. She has become an unofficial leader of those seeking the truth about the [[antelope-springs-incident|Antelope Springs Incident]].

"I'm not a conspiracy theorist," she has said. "I'm a widow who wants to know how her husband died. If the government has nothing to hide, why won't they release the records?"
