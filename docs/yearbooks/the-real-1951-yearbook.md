# The Real 1951 Yearbook

## Introduction

Nineteen fifty-one was the year America learned to live with war it could not win and fear it could not escape.

The Korean conflict that had begun with desperate retreat and ended 1950 with Chinese intervention settled into a grinding stalemate. By summer, the front stabilized near the 38th parallel—roughly where it had started. Armistice talks began in July and would drag on for two more years. American boys continued dying in hills with Korean names, but the headlines moved on. This was the new reality: a war that could not be won, could not be abandoned, could only be endured.

The year's most dramatic moment came in April, when President Harry Truman fired General Douglas MacArthur. The legendary commander had defied presidential orders, publicly advocated expanding the war into China, and sought Congressional support for his position. Truman's dismissal shocked the nation—MacArthur returned to parades and a hero's address to Congress—but the subsequent Senate hearings confirmed the principle that elected civilians, not generals, make American policy. "Old soldiers never die," MacArthur said. "They just fade away." And so, eventually, he did.

At home, fear of Communist subversion intensified. The Rosenbergs were convicted and sentenced to death for passing atomic secrets to the Soviets. Senator Joseph McCarthy attacked even George Marshall as part of a vast conspiracy. HUAC launched its second Hollywood investigation. Loyalty oaths spread. Careers were destroyed by accusation. The Supreme Court upheld convictions of Communist Party leaders. The Korean War validated the fears—Communism was a shooting war now—but the methods of the Red Scare would eventually discredit themselves.

Yet this was also the year television came of age. In March, Senator Estes Kefauver's crime hearings captivated 30 million Americans—more than watched the World Series—as organized crime was exposed to public view for the first time. Schools dismissed students to watch. The medium's power was confirmed when *I Love Lucy* debuted in October and immediately became the most popular program in America. By year's end, half of American households owned a television. The way Americans entertained themselves, informed themselves, and sold to themselves was transformed forever.

In baseball, Bobby Thomson's "Shot Heard 'Round the World" gave the sport its most dramatic moment—a walk-off homer to win the pennant after the Giants had trailed by 13½ games in August. In boxing, 37-year-old Jersey Joe Walcott became the oldest man to win the heavyweight title, while young Rocky Marciano knocked out his hero Joe Louis and cried in the loser's dressing room. On Broadway, *The King and I* opened to acclaim. In bookstores, J.D. Salinger's *The Catcher in the Rye* became an instant classic that would speak to teenage alienation for generations.

The atomic age advanced relentlessly. Nuclear tests in Nevada and the Pacific proved concepts that would lead to the hydrogen bomb. In December, a reactor in Idaho produced electricity from nuclear fission for the first time. The future was arriving—terrifying and promising in equal measure.

The 22nd Amendment, ratified in February, ensured no president would serve more than two terms—a response to FDR's four terms, a safeguard that would shape American politics forever.

Nineteen fifty-one was a year of stalemate—in Korea, in politics, in the Cold War itself. But within that stalemate, the future was taking shape: television, the hydrogen bomb, the recognition that the postwar world would be defined by permanent struggle rather than clear victory. Americans were learning to live with anxiety as a constant companion. The certainties of World War II were gone. In their place: a television in every living room, a war that wouldn't end, and the knowledge that the atom could destroy everything.

This is the story of that year.

## I. Everyday Life in America

### Cost of Living

The average American family earned approximately $3,700 in 1951—up $400 from the previous year. The minimum wage stood between $0.75 and $1.00 per hour. A dollar in 1951 would be worth roughly $10 in 2020, giving context to the era's modest prices.

**Typical Prices:**
- Gallon of gasoline: $0.23
- Loaf of bread: $0.18
- Dozen eggs: $0.61
- Half gallon of milk: $0.43
- Pound of T-bone steak: $0.95
- Pound of coffee: $0.93
- New car: approximately $1,500
- New home: $7,000–$9,000
- Television set (12½" screen): $149.95
- Three-minute phone call, Washington DC to California: $2.50

The minimum cost of a payphone call in New York doubled from 5 cents to 10 cents.

### Home & Family

The suburban boom continued at full speed. Young families—many headed by World War II veterans using GI Bill benefits—flooded into new developments. New houses were typically under 1,000 square feet, and most buyers were first-time homeowners in their early twenties with a single income.

The nuclear family ideal dominated American life: father working, mother keeping house, and children filling bedrooms of identical ranch homes. Gender roles remained rigid—women who had worked in wartime factories had largely returned to domestic life. The expectation was marriage, children, homemaking.

Television ownership exploded. By mid-1951, nearly half of American households owned a TV set—up from just 9% two years earlier. The television became the new hearth around which family life revolved.

### Work Life

Jobs were readily available, filled by a generation of eager-to-work veterans. More and more workers joined labor unions, which negotiated for pay raises, better working conditions, and health and retirement benefits. The "man in the gray flannel suit" emerged as both a symbol and a reality—conservative, conformist, forward-thinking within narrow bounds.

### Fashion & Style

**Women:** Designers focused on sleeve innovations—some swelled dramatically, others fanned out in pagoda ruffles. The "American Look" emphasized practical, casual clothing. Hats remained essential: pillboxes, veiled fascinators, Juliette caps, pancake hats, and large-brimmed straw hats for summer. Complete outfits included coordinated costume jewelry, gloves, and boxy handbags.

**Men:** Conservative, baggy suits in muted colors with narrow ties dominated professional life. For leisure, men adopted lightweight sports coats, colored shirts, and trousers. Hawaiian shirts and knee-length shorts became summer staples. The biggest fashion revolution came from Marlon Brando in *A Streetcar Named Desire*—his T-shirt worn as outerwear became "visual shorthand for rebellion." Grey, brown, and dark blue dominated, with silk and Donegal tweed the preferred fabrics for those who could afford quality.

### Health & Medicine

Polio remained the terror of every American parent. Summer brought epidemics—iron lungs filled hospital wards, and children who ran and played one day could be paralyzed the next. In 1951, researchers confirmed three immunologically distinct types of poliovirus, essential groundwork for developing a vaccine. William Hammon at the University of Pittsburgh began clinical trials of anti-polio serum, involving nearly 55,000 children.

The Epidemic Intelligence Service (EIS) was created in 1951 by Dr. Alexander D. Langmuir to aid states in controlling communicable diseases. Though initially part of biological warfare defense efforts during the Korean War, the EIS would become the foundation of modern epidemiological response.

### Social Norms & Expectations

Conformity was the watchword. Dating, courtship, and marriage followed prescribed patterns. Church attendance remained high. The Korean War cast its shadow—sons were called to serve, Gold Star families emerged, and the anxiety of conflict touched every community. McCarthy's investigations made everyone aware that accusations of disloyalty could destroy lives and careers overnight.

## II. Entertainment & Culture

### Film

The 23rd Academy Awards ceremony on March 29, 1951, honored films of 1950. *All About Eve* received a record 14 nominations (surpassing *Gone with the Wind*'s 13) and won six Oscars including Best Picture. Writer/director Joseph L. Mankiewicz won his second consecutive Best Director and Best Adapted Screenplay awards—an unprecedented achievement. Judy Holliday won Best Actress for *Born Yesterday*.

The 24th Academy Awards (honoring 1951 films) would recognize Humphrey Bogart for *The African Queen* and Vivien Leigh for *A Streetcar Named Desire*. *Rashomon*, Akira Kurosawa's masterpiece, won the Golden Lion at Venice in September and would receive an honorary Oscar, bringing worldwide attention to Japanese cinema.

**Notable 1951 Films:**
- *A Streetcar Named Desire* (Vivien Leigh, Marlon Brando)
- *The African Queen* (Humphrey Bogart, Katharine Hepburn)
- *A Place in the Sun* (Elizabeth Taylor, Montgomery Clift)
- Walt Disney's *Alice in Wonderland* (July 26 premiere—initially a disappointment, later a cult classic)

The House Un-American Activities Committee investigation into Communism in the film industry began winding down after four years, though the blacklist continued to destroy careers. In 1951, HUAC launched a second investigation of communism in Hollywood under Democratic Congressional control.

In April, the French magazine *Cahiers du cinéma* was first published, beginning a new era of film criticism.

### Television

The 1951-52 television season marked a watershed moment. **I Love Lucy** debuted on CBS in October and became an immediate sensation, revolutionizing the medium. The sitcom starring Lucille Ball and Desi Arnaz was shot before a studio audience using three cameras—a technique that would define television production for decades. At its peak, *I Love Lucy* achieved a 67.3 rating, meaning more than two-thirds of TV homes tuned in.

**Top Programs (1951-52 Season):**
1. *I Love Lucy* (CBS) - 68.2 rating
2. Boxing (NBC) - 67.0 rating
3. *Arthur Godfrey's Talent Scouts* (CBS) - 62.6 rating
4. *The Milton Berle Show* (NBC)

**Milton Berle** ("Mr. Television," "Uncle Bernie") still dominated with *Texaco Star Theater*—his vaudeville-inspired variety show of comedy, music, and outrageous costumes. Movie ticket sales dipped when his show aired; restaurants emptied before air time. Detroit reportedly saw water reservoir levels drop when he signed off as everyone rushed to bathrooms.

Other notable television debuts:
- *Dragnet* premiered on NBC
- *The Today Show* premiered on NBC

### Music & Radio

The music industry was in transition. Smooth, low-key crooning gave way to more emotive singing. A&R visionary Mitch Miller mined country music for pop hits. Les Paul pushed studio technology to new levels. Nat "King" Cole targeted teen audiences and sold millions.

**Top Songs of 1951:**
1. "Too Young" - Nat King Cole
2. "Because of You" - Tony Bennett
3. "How High the Moon" - Les Paul & Mary Ford
4. "Come On-a My House" - Rosemary Clooney
5. "Be My Love" - Mario Lanza
6. "Cry" - Johnnie Ray & The Four Lads
7. "Slow Poke" - Pee Wee King

Patti Page continued her dominance as one of the decade's most successful recording stars.

Billboard published four separate singles charts in 1951: Best Sellers in Stores, Most Played by Jockeys, Most Played in Jukeboxes, and the Honor Roll of Hits (a composite that served as the lead chart until the Hot 100's introduction in 1958).

### Theater

**The King and I** opened on Broadway on March 29, 1951, at the St. James Theatre, starring Gertrude Lawrence as Anna and the relatively unknown Yul Brynner as the King. Critics inevitably compared it to Rodgers and Hammerstein's previous triumph, *South Pacific* (which continued its run after opening in 1949). The original production won five Tony Awards and ran for 1,246 performances.

Other 1951 Broadway musicals:
- *Make a Wish* (April 18)
- *Two on the Aisle* (July 19)
- *Top Banana* (November 1)
- *Paint Your Wagon* (November 12)

### Cultural Phenomena & Fads

**Comic Strips:**
- **Dennis the Menace** debuted in 1951, created by Hank Ketcham, starting in just 16 newspapers but quickly becoming a nationwide phenomenon
- **Beetle Bailey** joined the Army in 1951 (after debuting as a college student in 1950), relocating to the fictional Camp Swampy
- **Peanuts** (Charles Schulz) continued building its audience after its 1950 debut
- **Brenda Starr** reached peak popularity in Sunday newspapers

## III. Sports

### Baseball

The 1951 baseball season culminated in one of the most dramatic moments in sports history: **Bobby Thomson's "Shot Heard 'Round the World."**

The New York Giants had been 13½ games behind the Brooklyn Dodgers in mid-August—seemingly out of the pennant race. But they won 16 games in a row and finished 37 of their last 44 games as winners, forcing a three-game playoff. On October 3, 1951, at the Polo Grounds, trailing 4-2 in the bottom of the ninth with two runners on base, Thomson hit a walk-off three-run home run off Ralph Branca.

Russ Hodges' radio call became perhaps the most famous in sports broadcasting history. Remarkably, no station was routinely recording the game—the call survives only because a Brooklyn fan named Lawrence Goldberg asked his mother to tape-record the last half-inning while he was at work.

Fifty years later, a Wall Street Journal investigation revealed the Giants' comeback may have benefited from a sign-stealing scheme—but that knowledge came long after the magic had been made.

**1951 World Series:** The Giants lost to the New York Yankees in six games. The Yankees won their third consecutive championship.

### Boxing

**Heavyweight Championship:** Jersey Joe Walcott defeated Ezzard Charles on July 18, 1951, at Forbes Field in Pittsburgh. Walcott, a 9-1 underdog, knocked Charles out with a left hook 55 seconds into the seventh round. At 37, Walcott became the oldest boxer to win the World Heavyweight Championship—a record that would stand until George Foreman's 1994 comeback at age 45.

The Ring named Walcott vs. Charles the Fight of the Year. Walcott also won the Edward J. Neil Trophy as Fighter of the Year.

**Rocky Marciano's Rise:** The undefeated contender continued his climb, knocking out Red Layne in July. On October 26, 1951, Marciano faced his idol, former champion Joe Louis, and knocked him out in the eighth round. Marciano was so upset about beating his hero that he cried in Louis's dressing room afterward. The fight had originally been scheduled for the Polo Grounds but was moved to Madison Square Garden when the Giants' unexpected pennant run took over the venue.

### Football

**Rose Bowl (January 1, 1951):** Michigan defeated California 14-6. Don Dufek was named Player of the Game.

**1951 College Football Season:** Dick Kazmaier of Princeton won the Heisman Trophy and Maxwell Award. Multiple teams claimed the national championship: Tennessee (unbeaten, ranked #1 in AP and UP polls) and Maryland (10-0, including a 28-13 Sugar Bowl victory over Tennessee).

**NFL:** The Pro Bowl, the league's all-star game, was instituted in 1951.

### Notable Athletes of 1951

- Bobby Thomson (baseball) - The Shot Heard 'Round the World
- Jersey Joe Walcott (boxing) - Heavyweight champion
- Rocky Marciano (boxing) - Rising contender
- Joe DiMaggio (baseball) - Final season
- Dick Kazmaier (football) - Heisman Trophy winner

## IV. Business & Commerce

### Major Corporations

**AT&T** became the first American corporation to have one million stockholders in 1951.

**General Motors** remained the world's largest company, dominating the automobile market with over 50% share. Between direct employees and suppliers, GM was responsible for more than 3 million American jobs.

**Du Pont** employed one-third more chemists than all of America's universities combined.

Corporate mergers accelerated:
- Chase National Bank + Bank of Manhattan → Chase-Manhattan
- Remington-Rand + Sperry Corporation → Sperry Rand
- Nash + Hudson → later absorbed by Chrysler

Among the decade's largest employers: GM, Chrysler, US Steel, Standard Oil of New Jersey, Amoco, Goodyear, and Firestone.

### Consumer Products & Advertising

Television advertising transformed how products reached consumers. Companies were spending approximately $53 per year for each American on advertising—annual marketing spending would double during the decade from $5.7 billion (1950) to $12 billion (1960).

Time payments (installment plans with interest) became common for major acquisitions: cars, refrigerators, ovens, dishwashers, dryers, and record players.

The Diners' Club card, introduced in 1950, continued spreading as America's first credit card.

### Economic Trends

The Korean War drove defense spending higher. Steel production increased. The economy, already strong from postwar recovery, accelerated further. Small businesses accounted for roughly 58% of total domestic output.

## V. Organizations

### Veterans Organizations

The American Legion and Veterans of Foreign Wars remained politically powerful, with posts in every town. The Korean War created a new generation of veterans.

### Labor Unions

Union membership peaked in the early 1950s, with over 30% of American workers belonging to unions. The major labor federations negotiated pay raises, benefits, and improved working conditions. The political influence of organized labor was at its height.

### Civic Organizations

Rotary, Kiwanis, Lions, and similar service organizations thrived. Church attendance remained high. The PTA expanded alongside the suburban boom and baby boom.

## VI. Domestic Politics

### Federal Government

**President Harry S. Truman** remained in office, his popularity worn down by the Korean War stalemate and the firing of MacArthur. The 22nd Amendment—limiting presidents to two terms—was ratified on February 27, 1951. Though it exempted Truman (the incumbent), he would choose not to seek reelection in 1952.

### The MacArthur Crisis

The year's defining political drama came on **April 11, 1951**, when Truman fired General Douglas MacArthur as commander of UN forces in Korea. MacArthur had repeatedly defied presidential orders, publicly advocating for expanding the war into China, using atomic weapons, and employing Nationalist Chinese forces from Taiwan.

The final straw came when MacArthur wrote to House Minority Leader Joseph Martin, arguing for military victory over Truman's containment policy. Martin read the letter on the House floor on April 5. Six days later, Truman relieved MacArthur.

MacArthur returned to a hero's welcome—parades in his honor, an address to Congress ("Old soldiers never die, they just fade away"), and momentary talk of a presidential run. But subsequent Senate hearings undercut his position. General Omar Bradley, chairman of the Joint Chiefs, flatly rejected MacArthur's strategy: "In the opinion of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, this strategy would involve us in the wrong war, in the wrong place, at the wrong time and with the wrong enemy."

The crisis affirmed civilian control of the military—the principle Truman invoked when he said: "If there is one basic element in our Constitution, it is civilian control of the military."

### McCarthyism and the Red Scare

Senator Joseph McCarthy's crusade continued at full intensity. In June 1951, McCarthy denounced George Marshall and Dean Acheson as part of "a conspiracy so immense" as to dwarf any other in history. HUAC launched its second Hollywood investigation.

**The Rosenberg Trial:** Julius and Ethel Rosenberg were tried for conspiracy to commit espionage beginning March 6, 1951. On March 29, they were convicted. On April 5, Judge Irving Kaufman sentenced both to death—they would be executed on June 19, 1953, the first American civilians executed for peacetime espionage conspiracy.

The Supreme Court's 6-2 decision in *Dennis v. United States* (1951) upheld the convictions of Communist Party leaders for advocating violent overthrow of the government. Tennessee enacted the death penalty for such advocacy.

### 22nd Amendment

Ratified on **February 27, 1951**, the Twenty-second Amendment limits presidents to two terms. It was a direct response to Franklin Roosevelt's four terms (1932-1944). Minnesota completed the ratification. Though Truman was exempted, the amendment would affect every subsequent president.

## VII. International Affairs & Military

### The Korean War

The war that began in June 1950 continued through 1951, but the nature of the conflict changed dramatically.

**January-April 1951:** Chinese forces pushed UN troops south of the 38th parallel. Seoul changed hands for the fourth time. General Matthew Ridgway stabilized the front and launched Operation Ripper, recapturing Seoul in March.

**April 11:** Truman fired MacArthur (see Domestic Politics).

**April-May 1951:** China's Fifth Phase Offensive threw 700,000 troops against UN lines. The Battles of the Imjin River and Kapyong (April 22-25) blunted the assault. Chinese casualties were catastrophic—40,000-60,000 compared to 4,000 UN casualties, a ratio of 10:1 or 15:1.

**July 1951 onward:** The front stabilized near the 38th parallel. The war became a stalemate that would last until the armistice of 1953.

**Armistice Talks:** Negotiations began July 10, 1951, at Kaesong, with Vice Admiral Charles Turner Joy representing the UN and General Nam Il representing North Korea. A five-part agenda was agreed upon July 26. In October, talks moved to Panmunjom. The main sticking point—the fate of prisoners of war who refused repatriation—would not be resolved for two more years.

**Casualties:** American forces would suffer nearly 37,000 killed and 92,000 wounded throughout the war. Notably, 45% of American casualties occurred after armistice negotiations began. By late 1951, monthly UN casualties dropped from 20,000 (October) to under 3,000 (December 1951-April 1952).

### International Treaties

**ANZUS Treaty (September 1, 1951):** Australia, New Zealand, and the United States signed a security treaty in San Francisco. The pact addressed Antipodean concerns about Japanese rearmament and Soviet expansion in the Pacific. Significantly, Britain was excluded—the first time Australia formed a political alliance without British involvement.

**Treaty of San Francisco (September 8, 1951):** Signed by 49 nations, this peace treaty formally ended World War II with Japan, ending the military occupation and re-establishing peaceful relations.

**U.S.-Japan Security Treaty (September 8, 1951):** Allowed American forces to be stationed on Japanese territory.

### Cold War Context

Stalin ruled the Soviet Union. Mao led China. The Sino-Soviet alliance was at its height—in 1951, China and the Soviet Union signed an agreement exchanging uranium ore for technical assistance in nuclear weapons development.

## VIII. Science, Technology & Innovation

### Nuclear Developments

**Nevada Test Site:** The U.S. conducted its first Nevada nuclear detonation on January 27, 1951 (Operation Ranger Shot Able). Multiple tests followed through the year.

**Operation Greenhouse (Pacific, Spring 1951):** Tests at Enewetak Atoll advanced thermonuclear weapons development. The "George" shot (May 8-9) was the first successful release of nuclear fusion energy, yielding 225 kilotons and proving the concept that would lead to the hydrogen bomb.

**The Teller-Ulam Breakthrough:** In March 1951, Edward Teller and Stanislaw Ulam developed a promising H-bomb design. At a June meeting in Oppenheimer's office at Princeton's Institute for Advanced Study, scientists concluded the design was technically sound.

**Operation Buster-Jangle (Fall 1951):** Military personnel participated in Operation Desert Rock, simulating activity on a nuclear battlefield. Thousands of troops witnessed atmospheric nuclear tests.

**First Nuclear Electricity (December 20, 1951):** The Experimental Breeder Reactor (EBR-1) at Argonne National Laboratory in Idaho became the first nuclear reactor to produce electricity.

### Medicine

**Polio Research:** Scientists confirmed three immunologically distinct types of poliovirus. William Hammon began clinical trials of anti-polio serum involving 55,000 children. The groundwork was being laid for the vaccines that would come later in the decade.

**Epidemic Intelligence Service:** Dr. Alexander Langmuir created the EIS program, initially for biological warfare detection during the Korean War but destined to become the foundation of modern epidemic response.

### Aviation & Space

Test pilots at Edwards Air Force Base continued pushing the boundaries of flight. The sound barrier, broken in 1947, was now routine—the question was how much faster and higher aircraft could go.

## XI. The Big Stories

### The MacArthur Firing

When Truman relieved Douglas MacArthur of command on April 11, 1951, it shocked the American political system and astonished the world. Much of the world didn't realize the president had the power to fire a five-star general; much of America didn't realize Truman had the nerve.

The conflict was fundamental: MacArthur believed he could win the war if Truman would let him strike at China; Truman feared a wider war, perhaps atomic war. MacArthur defied orders, sought Congressional support for his position, and forced the president's hand.

MacArthur returned to parades and a speech to Congress. But the Senate hearings that followed undercut him—the Joint Chiefs contradicted his claims of their support, and General Bradley's declaration that MacArthur's strategy would lead to "the wrong war, in the wrong place, at the wrong time and with the wrong enemy" became definitive.

The crisis affirmed that elected civilians, not generals, make American policy—a principle tested as never before.

### The Kefauver Crime Hearings

In March 1951, television came of age. Senator Estes Kefauver's Special Committee on Organized Crime broadcast hearings that captivated 30 million Americans—more than watched the World Series. Schools dismissed students to watch. Blood bank donations rose 100% when facilities installed televisions.

For the first time, Americans saw the hidden world of the Mafia: Frank Costello's nervous hands (he refused to let cameras show his face), Virginia Hill's glamorous testimony, gangsters invoking the Fifth Amendment. The hearings revealed a national crime syndicate coordinating operations across cities, corrupting politicians and police.

The Kefauver hearings demonstrated television's power to focus national attention and made their chairman a presidential contender. They also introduced Americans to terms like "Mafia" and "capo di tutti capi."

### The Korean War Stalemate

The Korean War transformed in 1951 from desperate retreat and dramatic counteroffensive to grinding stalemate. After MacArthur's Inchon triumph and China's intervention in 1950, the front stabilized near the 38th parallel by summer 1951.

Armistice talks began in July—and would continue for two years. The main issue: prisoners of war who refused repatriation. Meanwhile, fighting continued. The battles of Bloody Ridge, the Punchbowl, and Heartbreak Ridge produced 60,000 UN casualties (including 22,000 Americans) for minimal territorial gains.

The stalemate wore down public patience and Truman's approval rating. Americans wanted victory or withdrawal—not a war that "cannot be won, cannot be abandoned, can only be endured."

### Television's Triumph

By 1951, television ownership approached 50% of American households—up from 9% just two years earlier. The medium's power was demonstrated by the Kefauver hearings and confirmed by *I Love Lucy*'s October debut.

Lucille Ball and Desi Arnaz's sitcom wasn't just popular—it transformed how television was made. Three-camera filming, live studio audiences, and high production values created a template for decades of television comedy. At its peak, two-thirds of TV households tuned in.

Milton Berle remained "Mr. Television," but the medium was moving beyond vaudeville variety. Drama, news, sports—everything was being reshaped by the small screen. Movie attendance dropped. Radio evolved. The entertainment industry would never be the same.

### The Red Scare Continues

McCarthyism reached full intensity in 1951. The Rosenbergs were convicted and sentenced to death. HUAC launched its second Hollywood investigation. McCarthy himself attacked even George Marshall as a Communist conspirator. The Supreme Court upheld convictions of Communist Party leaders in *Dennis v. United States*.

Loyalty oaths spread through government and industry. Careers were destroyed by accusation. The blacklist grew. Fear of subversion—sometimes justified, often paranoid—shaped American life.

The Korean War validated the fears: Communism was not an abstraction but a shooting war. But the methods of McCarthy and others would eventually discredit themselves. For now, in 1951, the Red Scare's grip was firm.
